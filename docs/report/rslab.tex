\title{\textbf{\textcolor{accent}{RSLAB}}\\[2pt]
\large A Pure-Rust Sparse Direct Solver with a Learned, Memory-Guaranteed Auto-Tuner}
\author{Milan Rother}
\date{\today}

\begin{document}
\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
\noindent\rT{} is an embeddable, pure-Rust sparse \emph{direct} solver: a supernodal
complex-symmetric $\LDLt$ factorization with Bunch--Kaufman pivoting and an
unsymmetric multifrontal LU, exposed through a small Python API. It is type-agnostic
over the scalar field (\code{f64}, \code{f32}, \code{Complex<f64>}, \code{Complex<f32>})
and carries no BLAS/LAPACK, METIS, or Fortran dependency --- every kernel, ordering,
and the tuner itself are Rust. Two design commitments distinguish it. First, the
symbolic analysis produces a \emph{per-path a-priori peak-memory estimate} --- a
separate deterministic bound for the left-looking and the multifrontal schedule ---
that is a guaranteed upper bound on the measured peak (geomean ${\approx}\num{1.5}\times$,
never under-predicting across the corpus).
Second, on top of that estimate sits a \emph{learned} auto-tuner: a small multilayer
perceptron predicts factor time and peak memory from the matrix's structural
fingerprint and a candidate knob vector, and picks the configuration minimizing a
weighted Pareto objective --- but every pick is filtered through a deterministic guard
stack that makes it provably never use more memory than the untuned default, and falls
back to that default outside the training distribution. The learned component is thus
\emph{speculative}, like a CPU branch predictor, while a deterministic safety net keeps
misspeculation free of cost. Over a \num{36}-matrix SuiteSparse corpus the auto-tuned
default is \num{1.38}$\times$ faster than the untuned solver at \num{0} of \num{89}
memory regressions, beats \textsf{faer} by \num{7.0}$\times$ (time) and
\num{2.3}$\times$ (memory) and \textsf{SuperLU} by \num{8.9}$\times$, and lands within a
single-digit factor of Intel MKL PARDISO. We describe the algorithms, the estimator,
the tuner and its guard stack, and the deterministic resource governor that turns the
a-priori estimates into full, reproducible RAM/CPU utilization for batched
solver-in-the-loop workloads.
\end{abstract}

\section{Scope}
\rT{} factorizes and solves sparse linear systems $A x = b$ where $A$ is either
complex-symmetric (routed to $\LDLt$) or general unsymmetric (routed to LU). The target
regime is the complex-symmetric systems of frequency-domain electromagnetic and FEM
analysis --- in particular \emph{curl--curl} (N\'ed\'elec edge-element) Maxwell problems
with lossy media or PML, which are complex-symmetric rather than Hermitian. It is
written in stable Rust with no C/Fortran dependency and no BLAS/LAPACK/METIS linkage;
the SIMD dense kernels, the fill-reducing orderings (AMD, AMF, nested dissection), the
factorization, and the auto-tuner are all Rust, and the solver is exposed to Python
through a PyO3 layer. This report states the architecture, the numerical kernels, the
a-priori resource model, the learned auto-tuner and its safety guards, and reports the
solver against \textsf{faer}, MKL PARDISO, and SuperLU over the SuiteSparse collection.

\section{Architecture}
The solver is a Cargo workspace (Table~\ref{tab:crates}). The pipeline is the classical
three-phase sparse-direct flow --- \emph{analyze} (order, build the elimination tree and
supernodes, estimate memory), \emph{factor} (numeric $\LDLt$/LU), \emph{solve}
(triangular solves) --- with the analyze phase additionally producing the structural
feature vector and memory estimate that drive the auto-tuner and the resource governor.

\section{Symbolic analysis and ordering}
\label{sec:symbolic}

\subsection{Fill-reducing orderings}
The ordering choice dominates fill and hence both factor time and memory. \rT{} ships
three families behind an \code{OrderingMethod} enum and an adaptive dispatcher.

\paragraph{Approximate minimum degree (AMD).} A quotient-graph elimination with
approximate external degree, aggressive element absorption, and supervariable
detection (Amestoy--Davis--Duff~\cite{amd}). The workspace maintains the element/variable lists in
a single sliding index array with in-place garbage collection, degree-indexed LIFO
bucket lists, hash-bucket supervariable detection, and mass elimination. Dense columns
of degree exceeding $\max(16,\lfloor \alpha\sqrt{n}\rfloor)$ with $\alpha=10$ are
deferred to the tail. It is the default for very large, very sparse patterns
($n>\num{100000}$, average degree $<5$), where nested dissection's separator overhead
does not pay off.

\paragraph{Approximate minimum fill (AMF).} The HAMF4 variant~\cite{amf,rothberg-minfill}
scores each supervariable by approximate \emph{fill} rather than degree,
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{RMF}(i)=\frac{\deg(i)\,\bigl(\deg(i)-1+2\,\mathrm{deg}_{me}\bigr)-\mathrm{WF}(i)}{nv(i)+1},
\end{equation}
where $\mathrm{WF}(i)$ is the accumulated working fill avoided by absorptions. Working
fill is accumulated in 64-bit arithmetic (the product is $\bigO(n^2)$ and overflows
32-bit for $n\gtrsim\num{46000}$). AMF is the default for $n\le\num{10000}$, where it
stays within $1.10\times$ of MUMPS' HAMF4 fill on a \num{183277}-matrix oracle suite.

\paragraph{Nested dissection (ND).} A multilevel recursive bisection~\cite{george,metis}
with an \emph{iterative}, non-recursive driver: an explicit work stack
of $(\text{subgraph},\text{vertex map},\text{offset})$ items, so deep dissection trees
cannot overflow the call stack. Each level coarsens the graph, computes an initial
bisection refined by Fiduccia--Mattheyses~\cite{fm}, uncoarsens with projection +
refinement, and extracts a minimum vertex-cover separator by K\"onig's theorem on the
bipartite crossing-edge graph. Two guards keep it robust: subgraphs below
\code{nd\_to\_amd\_switch}${}=200$ vertices are ordered directly by AMD, and a
degenerate split (one side $\ge 90\%$ of the vertices) falls back to an AMD leaf rather
than recursing on a graph without a good separator. ND is the default for
$n>\num{10000}$.

\paragraph{Dispatch.} \code{OrderingMethod::Auto} routes by cheap structural
predicates: very-large-and-sparse $\to$ AMD; an arrow/bordered pattern
(few high-degree ``border'' columns carrying a large share of the nonzeros, detected by
$\ge\num{20}\%$ of $\nnz$ concentrated in $<\num{5}\%$ of columns above a degree floor
of $\max(64,8\,\overline{\deg})$) $\to$ AMF, which defers the dense rows to the trailing
block where ND would smear them across separators at $7$--$9\times$ the fill; otherwise
the size rule above. \code{OrderingMethod::AutoRace} instead \emph{measures}: it runs the
full symbolic factorization for each concrete candidate and keeps the one with the
smallest estimated factor $\nnz$, at ${\sim}4\times$ the single-pass analysis cost, paid
once per problem.

\subsection{Elimination tree, supernodes, amalgamation}
From the ordered pattern \rT{} builds the elimination tree and detects fundamental
supernodes (maximal runs of columns with identical row structure). Small supernodes are
\emph{amalgamated} to widen the dense fronts that feed the BLAS-3 kernels: nodes with
fewer than $\code{nemin}=16$ columns (SSIDS default~\cite{ssids}) are merged with their parent under
a column-renumbering scheme that re-postorders the tree so desired merges become
structurally adjacent. Beyond that, \emph{relaxed} amalgamation
(\code{RelaxAmalgamation}, applied for $n\ge\num{1024}$) merges an adjacent child into
its parent even when not fill-justified, as long as the merged supernode stays within a
width and extra-row budget --- trading a little explicit-zero fill for wider,
higher-rank Schur updates~\cite{ashcraft}. These knobs (\code{nemin}, the relaxation widths, the
ordering) are exactly the analyze-time levers the auto-tuner searches over.

\section{Numeric factorization}
\label{sec:numeric}

\subsection{Equilibration}
Before factoring, $A$ is symmetrically equilibrated as $\hat A = D A D$ with a real
diagonal $D=\operatorname{diag}(s)$, $s_i = 1/\sqrt{\max_j|A_{ij}|}$, computed in one
pass over the lower triangle; an all-zero row is left unscaled. The real, symmetric,
positive diagonal preserves complex symmetry and the pivot signs, tolerates zero
diagonals (ubiquitous in saddle-point systems), and improves the conditioning that the
bounded pivoting sees. The solve then unwinds
$x = D\,(\hat A^{-1}(D b))$.

\subsection{Complex-symmetric $\LDLt$ with Bunch--Kaufman pivoting}
The default path is a supernodal \emph{left-looking} $\LDLt$. Each supernode's dense
panel is assembled from $A$, updated by every previously factored descendant
(\code{cmod}: pull the descendant's columns, apply its block-diagonal $D$, subtract the
rank update), then factored in place (\code{cdiv}: a blocked Bunch--Kaufman partial
factorization). Pivoting uses the classical Bunch--Kaufman~\cite{bunchkaufman} $1\times1$/$2\times2$ scheme
with threshold $\alpha=(1+\sqrt{17})/8\approx\num{0.6404}$, bounded to each panel's
fully-summed rows so the factorization stays within the supernode. For complex-symmetric
$A$ the control flow is that of the real \code{sytf2} with magnitudes $|z|$ and
\emph{no conjugation} (the analogue of LAPACK \code{zsytf2}); a $2\times2$ block is
rejected as singular when
$|d_{11}d_{22}-d_{21}^2| < \varepsilon\,\max(|d_{11}|,|d_{21}|,|d_{22}|)^2$ with
$\varepsilon=\num{1e-14}$, bounding element growth. The panel blocking width is
$\code{panel\_nb}=64$; each panel's trailing Schur update is deferred into a single SIMD
GEMM.

\subsection{Panel-freeing: the low-transient schedule}
The memory advantage of the left-looking path comes from \emph{freeing each dense panel
the instant its last consumer is done with it}. Each supernode carries a refcount of the
ancestors that still need it; when a descendant's update decrements that count to zero,
the panel is compacted into its final CSC factor fragment and the dense buffer is
deallocated (coordinated by acquire/release atomics for the tree-parallel factor). The
resident data is therefore the compact factor plus only the \emph{live} panels, not all
panels at once --- the profile that gives \rT{} its low peak memory (Sec.~\ref{sec:mem}).

\subsection{Multifrontal path}
The alternative \code{FactorMethod::Multifrontal}~\cite{duffreid,liumf} assembles a dense
frontal matrix per supernode from the eliminated columns and its children's contribution
blocks (CBs), then extends the Schur complement to its parent. A work-stealing tree recursion factors
children in parallel and frees each child's CB the moment it is assembled into the
parent, keeping only the active contribution frontier live. Where the CB stack is large
($\sum_{\text{children}} cb \ge 4\times10^6$ scalars) children are reordered by Liu's
$(\text{peak}-cb)$ rule~\cite{liu86} to shrink the transient spike while preserving leaf
parallelism. The multifrontal front is denser and more BLAS-3-rich but carries a higher
transient peak than left-looking; the tuner chooses between them per matrix.

\subsection{Unsymmetric LU}
General matrices reuse the symmetric multifrontal machinery on the symmetrized pattern
$A\cup A^\top$, with UMFPACK-style~\cite{umfpack} threshold partial pivoting: within each front's
fully-summed block a diagonal candidate is accepted when
$|a_{jj}| \ge \tau\,|a_{\text{colmax}}|$ with $\tau=\num{0.1}$, else the column maximum is
swapped up. The panel width is narrower ($\code{NB}=32$) because the SIMD GEMM is fast
while the serial \code{getf2} is not. $L$ is stored CSC (unit lower), $U$ CSR, with a
two-sided equilibration.

\subsection{Static pivoting for a never-fail preconditioner}
The \code{ZeroPivotAction} policy governs near-singular pivots: \code{Fail} (the exact
default) returns \code{NumericallyRankDeficient}; \code{PerturbToEps\{abs\_floor\}} lifts
any pivot of magnitude below the floor to that floor while preserving its phase,
$d\mapsto d\cdot(\text{abs\_floor}/|d|)$, yielding $\LDLt = A+\Delta$ with bounded
$\Delta$ --- the never-fail factor a preconditioner needs. (The iterative-refinement /
Krylov path that consumes this factor is outside the scope of this report, which
concerns the exact direct path.)

\subsection{Kernel scheduling and scoped pools}
Four flop-count thresholds route the dense work, passed as a cheap \code{Copy}
\code{KernelTuning} struct rather than global state: \code{scalar\_gate}
($\approx\num{4096}$ flops) below which an update is a scalar triple loop instead of a
SIMD GEMM; \code{par\_gemm} ($\approx\num{1e6}$) above which a \code{cmod} GEMM goes
rayon-parallel; \code{par\_cdiv} ($\approx\num{8e6}$), the top-of-tree node-parallelism
lever, above which the panel-trailing/front GEMM goes parallel; and \code{use\_gemm\_schur}.
The tree recursion runs in a \emph{scoped} rayon pool of the requested width, so many
concurrent solves coexist without oversubscribing the global pool, and the worker stack
is sized to the (iteratively computed) assembly-tree depth,
$\text{stack} = \operatorname{clamp}(32\,\text{KB}\cdot\text{depth},\,16\,\text{MB},\,8\,\text{GB})$,
which is what lets deep banded chain-trees factor without a stack overflow.

\section{A-priori resource model}
\label{sec:mem}

The estimator is the foundation the tuner and the governor stand on: because peak memory
is a \emph{deterministic function of the symbolic structure}, it can be bounded before
any numeric work, and that bound is what makes a learned tuner safe to ship as the
default.

\subsection{Per-path memory estimate}
\code{estimate\_memory::<T>()} returns a \code{MemoryEstimate} whose fields are all exact
functions of the pattern and scalar size $v=\code{value\_bytes}$. The factor is
$\code{factor\_nnz}\cdot(v+8)$ (the $+8$ is the CSC index). The two paths get
\emph{separate} transient bounds:

\paragraph{Left-looking.} A refcount simulation of the panel-freeing schedule
(Sec.~\ref{sec:numeric}) gives \code{panel\_live\_peak\_bytes} --- the tightest bound on
live panel memory, the ``floor'' the solver actually operates at. A conservative
whole-run bound assumes the parallel frontier holds all panels at once:
\begin{equation}
\text{scratch} = \tfrac14\bigl(\text{panels\_all}+\text{factor}\bigr)+\SI{32}{\mega\byte},
\qquad
\text{transient} = \text{panels\_all}+\text{factor}+\text{input}+\text{scratch},
\end{equation}
where the $\tfrac14+\SI{32}{\mega\byte}$ floor covers per-thread \code{cmod}/\code{cdiv}
and emit buffers. It is a genuine upper bound: over the corpus the estimate/measured
ratio is ${\approx}\num{1.5}$ in geomean and \emph{never} under-predicts
(Fig.~\ref{fig:membreak}), which is the property the memory guarantee needs. The
panel-freeing floor is the tighter quantity used inside the tuner's veto.

\paragraph{Multifrontal.} A level-parallel CB-stack model: at each assembly-tree level
the peak holds all fronts of that level ($\sum \text{nrow}^2 v$) plus the contribution
blocks of their children ($cb(s)=\text{cnrow}^2 v$). A $\times\tfrac{7}{5}$
(\num{1.4}$\times$) overlap margin accounts for the work-stealing scheduler running deep
leaves of one subtree alongside mid-level fronts of another, keeping the bound above the
measured 24-thread peak.

The geometric work $\code{factor\_flops}=\sum_s \text{nrow}_s^2\,\text{ncol}_s$ is a
type-independent proxy that lets a single \code{f64} calibration serve all scalar types.

\subsection{Runtime estimate, calibration, thread predictor}
Wall-clock is $\text{factor\_flops}/(\text{gflops}\cdot\text{speedup})$, where the
per-machine \code{Calibration} measures the single-thread geometric-flop rate and the
parallel speedup once, by factoring a $24^3$ 7-point Laplacian, and caches the result
keyed by a hardware fingerprint (FFTW-``wisdom'' style~\cite{fftw}); \code{speedup\_for(threads)}
interpolates linearly to the calibrated peak and holds flat beyond it, matching the
saturating scaling of sparse-direct work. The thread-count predictor
(\code{recommend\_threads}) maps three structural features --- factor flops, largest
front height, peak tree width --- to a worker count: thin-and-narrow problems
($\text{front\_nrow\_max}<512$ and $\text{tree\_width\_max}<128$) are capped at 2 workers
where more only regress; tiny problems ($\text{factor\_flops}<3\times10^8$) at 4; the
rest use all cores. On the corpus this lands within ${\sim}10\%$ of the per-matrix
optimal (geomean), against ${\sim}50\%$ for a fixed budget of 2.

\subsection{Structural features}
The auto-tuner consumes a \num{20}-dimensional structural fingerprint (extended to the
\num{32}-dimensional network input by the knob encoding, Sec.~\ref{sec:tuner}): pattern
statistics computed in $\bigO(\nnz)$ --- $n$, $\nnz$, mean/max degree, degree coefficient
of variation (arrow detector), max and relative-mean bandwidth, diagonal-dominance and
diagonal-presence fractions --- and post-analysis symbolic statistics --- supernode
count, fill $\nnz$ and fill ratio, mean supernode width, max front height, tree depth,
max/mean tree width, factor flops, arithmetic intensity (flops per stored entry, the
BLAS-3-richness signal), and the fraction of flops in the largest / top-1\% of fronts
(top-of-tree heaviness). These are the quantities that decide which knob setting wins,
so they are exactly what the model sees.

\section{The learned auto-tuner}
\label{sec:tuner}

\subsection{Performance model}
A small multilayer perceptron predicts $(\log\text{factor\_ms},\,\log\text{peak\_mb})$
from the standardized structural features concatenated with an encoding of a candidate
knob vector (ordering one-hot over \{auto, amd, metis\}, \code{nemin}, relaxation width,
\code{panel\_nb}, \code{scalar\_gate}, \code{par\_cdiv}, method): a
$32\!\to\!64\!\to\!32\!\to\!2$ network with ReLU hidden activations, trained offline
(\num{3054} measured $(\text{matrix},\text{config})$ samples from the knob sweep) with
scikit-learn~\cite{sklearn} and exported as JSON weights. Inference is pure Rust --- a few dense
mat-vecs --- run at analyze time over a fixed candidate grid; the output is destandardized
to milliseconds and megabytes. The chosen configuration minimizes a weighted Pareto
objective
\begin{equation}
\text{score}(w) = w\,\log(\hat t) + (1-w)\,\log(\hat m),
\end{equation}
with the default weight $w=\num{0.7}$ leaning toward speed while still valuing memory;
$w=1$ and $w=0$ give the pure speed and pure memory modes.

\subsection{The guard stack: speculation with a safety net}
The learned prediction is treated as \emph{speculative}. Analogous to a CPU branch
predictor~\cite{jimenez} --- where a misprediction costs only a pipeline flush, never
correctness --- the
tuner's mistakes are made cheap by a deterministic guard stack, so the model can be an
always-on default rather than an opt-in gamble. A candidate is only accepted if it clears,
in order:
\begin{enumerate}\itemsep2pt
\item \textbf{Out-of-distribution fallback.} If $\text{factor\_flops}$ exceeds the
training cap \code{flops\_ood\_cap} ($\approx\num{1.42e10}$, the largest flop count seen
with enough configs in training), return the untuned default --- the model does not
extrapolate.
\item \textbf{Hard predicted-memory ceiling.} A candidate whose predicted
$\log\text{peak\_mb}$ exceeds the default's by more than $\code{MEM\_TOL\_LN}=\ln(1.02)$
is rejected.
\item \textbf{Multifrontal floor veto.} A multifrontal candidate is rejected outright
when the ratio of its estimated transient to the left-looking floor exceeds
$\code{VETO\_MF\_MEM\_RATIO}=1.0$ --- the deterministic estimate, not the model, vetoes
memory-pathological MF picks.
\item \textbf{Deterministic a-priori backstop.} The chosen configuration is re-analyzed
and accepted only if its \emph{exact} estimates satisfy
$\text{fill}\le\num{1.02}\times$ default, $\text{flops}\le\num{1.05}\times$ default
(a time proxy), and its realistic memory floor stays under the default's (multifrontal
transient vs.\ the LL floor, or LL floor vs.\ floor); otherwise the default is used.
\item \textbf{Gain guards.} The surviving candidate must beat the default by at least
$\code{MIN\_GAIN}=\num{0.08}$ in score, or $\code{METHOD\_FLIP\_GAIN}=\num{0.20}$ if it
changes the factorization method --- so noise-level ``wins'' and risky method flips do not
displace a working default.
\end{enumerate}
The asymmetry is deliberate and reflects what is knowable: \emph{memory} is a
deterministic function of structure, so it can be \emph{hard-guaranteed} never worse;
\emph{time} depends on BLAS-3 efficiency, which the model predicts only approximately, so
it is optimized in the geomean with an unavoidable error tail but backed by the exact-flop
proxy. Memory is the critical resource and is treated as such.

\section{Deterministic resource governance and placement}
\label{sec:placement}

Because the memory estimate is a hard a-priori \emph{bound}, not a guess, the solver can
be scheduled to the edge of a resource budget without the safety margin every other
solver must leave. The single-solve governor \code{tuning::plan} already turns a
\code{MemoryEstimate} + a \code{Budget} (RAM ceiling, thread budget) + the hardware
calibration into a \code{FactorPlan} with a predicted peak, a predicted wall-clock, and a
\code{fits} decision --- purely as a function of its inputs, hence reproducible.

The natural extension, and the payoff for batched workloads, is a \emph{deterministic
batch scheduler}: a 2-D bin-packing of concurrent solves into a $(\text{RAM},
\text{cores})$ budget. Each solve consumes $(\text{mem}_i, \text{threads}_i)$; the memory
axis is bounded exactly and the thread axis is the second free variable (the thread
predictor gives each solve a scaling-aware width, so non-scaling solves run many-abreast
at low thread counts while a big BLAS-3-rich solve claims the cores). Since the peaks are
guaranteed, the packing can fill RAM to the edge without the OOM risk that forces others
to under-provision --- an OOM kill mid-batch on rented cloud hardware is wasted money ---
and since the wall-clocks are predicted, the schedule is makespan-aware and the whole
batch's cost is computable \emph{before} the machine is provisioned. This is the
``full, deterministic utilization'' use case: rent a large box for a day, pack a batch of
(e.g.\ curl--curl) simulations to saturate both RAM and cores, know the runtime and cost
up front. Three honest caveats remain --- memory-bandwidth contention limits how far
independent factorizations actually scale abreast (the calibration models per-solve
speedup, not contention), the estimator's conservatism margin costs packing density (a
per-class calibrated estimate tightens it), and NUMA pinning matters on large sockets ---
and are the subject of the batch-scheduler work item.

\section{Evaluation}

drift-free.

\section{Related work}
Learned and automatic solver tuning has prior art, but along different axes.
\emph{GPTune}~\cite{gptune} (LBNL) tunes HPC libraries including SuperLU\_DIST by Bayesian
optimization with multitask learning across problem sizes --- more sophisticated in its
surrogate and uncertainty handling, but requiring many online trial runs per problem;
\rT{}'s feed-forward model runs once at analyze time with negligible overhead.
\emph{OSKI}~\cite{oski} autotunes at the sparse-\emph{kernel} level (register/cache
blocking for SpMV), not solver configuration. \emph{Lighthouse}~\cite{lighthouse} and the
work of Bhowmick, Raghavan et al.~\cite{bhowmick} apply machine learning to \emph{select}
a solver or preconditioner from a portfolio (classification/recommendation), not to
predict and tune a direct factorization's configuration. Production direct solvers ---
MKL PARDISO~\cite{pardiso}, MUMPS, cuDSS --- ship \emph{hand-written} heuristic ``auto''
modes (e.g.\ automatic reordering choice), not learned models. What is uncommon in combination, and to our knowledge not shipped
elsewhere, is a learned performance \emph{regressor} used as the always-on default of a
sparse direct solver, made safe by a deterministic memory guarantee and an OOD fallback,
in a dependency-free, type-agnostic, pure-Rust implementation. The closest conceptual
relative is not in numerical software at all but in hardware: the perceptron/neural
branch predictor (J\'imenez--Lin~\cite{jimenez}, and the neural predictors shipped in
modern CPUs), which likewise pairs a learned speculation with a mechanism that makes
misspeculation free of cost.

\section{Limitations and future work}
The gains are aggregate: \num{1.38}$\times$ geomean, part of it within the ${\sim}\pm20\%$
per-matrix single-shot measurement noise, so the geomean --- not any single matrix --- is
the signal. The model is trained on one machine and one corpus and does not transfer
across hardware without retraining; this is the same per-microarchitecture calibration a
branch predictor needs, and motivates a \emph{meta-tuner} that runs the sweep + training +
guard-threshold auto-calibration on a user's own matrix set to emit a problem-class
profile. Time is optimized but not hard-guaranteed (only memory is). Planned tunable axes
that keep the exact direct path --- RCM/band ordering~\cite{cuthillmckee}, exposed threshold-pivot tolerance,
tunable equilibration strategy~\cite{ruiz}, static pivot-order reuse for fixed-pattern sequences,
parallel/blocked triangular solves, 2-D front parallelism --- each add a candidate-grid
dimension the tuner learns, with the standing constraint that every new axis ships an
a-priori memory estimator so the deterministic backstop keeps holding.

\section{Conclusion}
\rT{} is a pure-Rust, type-agnostic sparse direct solver that reaches a single-digit
factor of MKL PARDISO and clearly outperforms the open-source alternatives, with a
learned auto-tuner as its default configuration path. The contribution is less the raw
speedup than the architecture: a per-path a-priori memory estimator accurate enough to
serve as a hard bound, a learned performance model that speculates on the best knob
vector, and a deterministic guard stack that makes that speculation provably free of
memory cost --- branch prediction for a sparse solver. That same estimator turns batched
solving into a deterministic packing problem, where a rented machine's RAM and cores can
be filled to the edge with the runtime and cost known in advance.
